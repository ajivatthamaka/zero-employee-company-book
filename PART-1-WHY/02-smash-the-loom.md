# Chapter 2: Smash the Loom

> "The factory of the future will have only two employees, a man and a dog. The man will be there to feed the dog. The dog will be there to keep the man from touching the equipment."
> — Warren Bennis

In 1811, English textile workers broke into factories at night and smashed weaving machines with sledgehammers. They called themselves Luddites, after a possibly fictional apprentice named Ned Ludd who had supposedly destroyed two stocking frames in a fit of rage.

The Luddites were skilled craftsmen who saw, correctly, that automated looms would destroy their livelihoods. A single power loom could do the work of several hand weavers.

They were right about the destruction. They were wrong about what came next.

Within a generation, the textile industry employed *more* people than before, in different roles. Machine operators, mechanics, factory supervisors, logistics coordinators, salespeople serving vastly expanded markets. The machines did not kill the industry. They killed one way of working and created another, far larger.

Two centuries later, we are having the same argument. Just with better machines.

## Ninety-Three Minutes per Model T

In 1913, Henry Ford introduced the moving assembly line at his Highland Park plant. Before: a skilled team took over twelve hours to assemble a single Model T. After: ninety-three minutes.

The craftsmen were furious. Years of honed skill, suddenly worthless. Any worker could learn one repetitive task in a few hours.

But Ford's assembly line did more than change how cars were built. It changed who could afford them. The Model T dropped from $850 in 1908 to $260 by 1925. Cars went from luxury goods to middle-class essentials. That explosion in demand created entire industries: gas stations, road construction, suburban development, insurance, repair shops, parts manufacturing, trucking, drive-in restaurants.

The assembly line eliminated certain jobs. It also created an economy so much larger that the net effect was enormously productive, for those who adapted.

## Nobody Misses the Typing Pool

If you worked in a large corporation in the 1970s, there was a room, sometimes an entire floor, called the typing pool. Rows of women (almost always women) sat at typewriters, transcribing handwritten documents and memos dictated by executives. A large company might employ dozens.

The personal computer killed the typing pool within a decade. By the mid-1990s, executives typed their own emails. The dedicated typist became a historical curiosity.

In the typing pool's place: word processing specialists, IT support teams, database administrators, desktop publishers, and eventually the entire digital knowledge economy. The computer did not reduce office work. It transformed *what* office work meant and spawned vastly more of it.

Those who learned the new tool thrived. Those who identified with the old one struggled. The difference was not talent. It was willingness to change.

## One Man, One Website, $13 Billion Gone

In 2000, the newspaper industry earned roughly $19.5 billion in classified advertising revenue in the United States. By 2012: $4.6 billion. About $13 billion, and the jobs attached to it, evaporated.

The cause was a website built by one person.

Craig Newmark started Craigslist in 1995 as an email list for San Francisco events. It grew into a classified platform that did, for free, what newspapers charged for. Why pay $300 for a help-wanted ad when Craigslist cost nothing?

Newspaper classified departments — salespeople, layout designers, phone operators — were gutted. Real people lost real jobs. Many newspapers went bankrupt.

But Craigslist did not eliminate the need to buy, sell, and find things. It eliminated the expensive middleman. And in the space it opened: eBay, Amazon Marketplace, Etsy, Facebook Marketplace, Indeed, Zillow, Airbnb. The classified ad industry did not shrink. It splintered into a hundred platforms, each creating new businesses, new jobs, new wealth.

One person with a website replaced a $13 billion industry. That was 1995 technology. Consider what one person with AI can do.

## More ATMs, More Tellers

When ATMs appeared in the 1970s, everyone predicted they would eliminate bank tellers. The logic seemed airtight: if a machine dispenses cash, why keep a human behind the counter?

Economist James Bessen studied what actually happened. Between 1980 and 2010, ATMs in the US grew from roughly 100,000 to 400,000. During the same period, bank tellers *also* grew, from about 500,000 to roughly 550,000.

ATMs reduced the tellers needed *per branch*, which made branches cheaper to open. Banks opened more locations. More locations needed tellers, fewer per branch, doing different work. Instead of counting cash all day, tellers shifted to customer service, relationship building, and sales. The role changed. It did not disappear.

AI will eliminate some roles outright. But the ATM story reminds us that automation reshapes work in ways hard to predict from the inside.

## Platforms Eat the Middlemen

The internet restructured the relationship between producers and consumers by eliminating the intermediaries who sat between them.

Travel agents gave way to Expedia and Booking.com. Bookstores to Amazon. Record labels were undercut by iTunes, then Spotify. Taxi dispatchers by Uber and Lyft. Real estate listings moved from agents to Zillow and Redfin.

In every case, an intermediary layer that existed because of *information asymmetry*, they knew something you did not, got flattened by technology that gave everyone the same information.

And in every case, new platforms created more economic activity than the old middlemen ever did. Amazon did not just replace bookstores. It built an e-commerce network that employs over 1.5 million people and enables millions of third-party sellers. Shopify alone powers over 4 million stores.

The internet did not shrink commerce. It removed friction and multiplied it.

AI is doing the same thing, but to *labor* instead of *information*. It is not removing barriers to accessing knowledge. It is removing barriers to getting work done.

## Same Arc, Every Time

Every wave of automation follows the same arc:

1. **A new technology emerges** that can do something humans currently do
2. **Panic and resistance** — "this will destroy everything"
3. **Disruption hits** — specific roles and industries suffer real damage
4. **New tools, new skills, new business models emerge**
5. **The market expands** beyond what anyone predicted
6. **New wealth accrues** to people who built with the new tools, not against them

The Luddites smashed machines. Ford built an empire. Newspaper classifieds died. Craigslist, eBay, and Airbnb were born. Typing pools vanished. The knowledge economy exploded.

We are at step 2 with AI. The panic is loud. The resistance is real. The disruption has started.

## Solo Founders Hold the Same Cards

Here is what separates the AI wave from every previous cycle: the tools are immediately accessible to individuals.

You did not need to own a factory to benefit from the assembly line, but you needed capital. You did not need a mainframe to benefit from personal computers, but you needed to buy one. Building an internet company took developers and servers. A mobile app took iOS expertise.

To build an AI company? A laptop and an internet connection. Paperclip is free. The AI models cost pennies per task. The infrastructure runs on your own machine.

This is the first major automation wave where a solo founder can reach for the same tools as a billion-dollar corporation on day one. No infrastructure moat. No capital barrier. The only advantage that matters is *knowledge and speed*, knowing what to build and building it before everyone else catches on.

Every automation wave produces its builders. This chapter was the history. The next is about the people already doing it.
